# Course Framework Thread

## 1. Master framework record

The course is mainly about how to analyze public policy through `policy mix`, `tool layering`, and `effectiveness assessment`.

### Core logic

- Do not analyze a single policy in isolation.
- Treat policy as a mix of instruments.
- Separate instruments into layers:
  - substantive legal-regulatory tools
  - procedural tools
  - implementation / organizational tools
  - informational / nodality tools
- Focus on interactions among tools:
  - complementarity
  - conflict / trade-off
  - overlap / redundancy
  - gap
- Evaluate the mix with an effectiveness lens:
  - problem-solving effectiveness
  - implementation and operational effectiveness
  - policy coherence and side-effect management
  - adaptive / governance effectiveness

### Main course contribution

- The course is not asking whether a policy exists.
- It is asking how policy mixes are assembled, how they interact, and why they become effective or ineffective.
- Complexity is normal, not exceptional.
- Policy failure may come from interaction problems rather than the absence of policy.
- Cross-department coordination matters as much as formal legal authority.

## 2. Second-layer mapping: Hong Kong pluvial flooding

### Why this case fits the course

- The case is not just an engineering issue.
- It is a governance and policy design issue.
- It shows mixed instruments, sectoral intersections, and possible implementation gaps.
- It is suitable for policy-mix analysis because flood control, water quality, urban development, and climate adaptation intersect.

### Main policy tension

- The key tension is between flood drainage logic and water-quality control logic.
- Flood governance prioritizes rapid conveyance, drainage capacity, and emergency response.
- Water pollution control prioritizes discharge control, licensing, and water-quality objectives.
- In extreme rainfall events, these logics can align in principle but still produce practical tension.

### Likely analytical use in the paper

- Use the case to show that Hong Kong’s flood governance is a layered toolkit, not a single policy.
- Use interaction analysis to identify where the toolkit is complementary and where it is counterproductive.
- Use effectiveness analysis to judge whether the mix works coherently in practice or only produces partial success.

## 3. What to remember for later writing

- The case should be framed as a sustainability governance problem, not only a drainage problem.
- The strongest analytical route is to explain why a policy mix may look strong on paper but remain partially effective in practice.
- The main explanatory candidates are:
  - objective tension
  - bureaucratic fragmentation
  - implementation gap
  - incomplete monitoring / partial evidence
  - limited cross-department coordination
