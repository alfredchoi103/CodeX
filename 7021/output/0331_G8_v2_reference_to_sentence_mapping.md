This sentence is broad and really contains several claims. It is better supported by a grouped citation than by one single paper.

Suggested grouped citation:
`(Ricci & Caratelli, 2017; Wang et al., 2024; Goda et al., 2023)`

### Suggested source 1

Ricci, O., & Caratelli, M. (2017). Financial literacy, trust and retirement planning. *Journal of Pension Economics & Finance, 16*(1), 43-64. https://doi.org/10.1017/S1474747215000177

- What this source is about:
  This paper examines how financial literacy and trust jointly affect retirement planning behavior. It argues that planning is shaped not only by knowledge, but also by whether people trust financial actors and institutions.
- Why this source fits this citation:
  Your sentence argues that financial education alone is not enough. This source directly supports that logic because it treats trust as an additional driver of retirement planning.
- Other viewpoints on the same issue:
  Some studies emphasize literacy and financial capability more strongly than trust. Others argue trust matters mainly when products are complex or when advice is mediated through institutions.

### Suggested source 2

Wang, H., Zhang, Y., et al. (2024). Rely on whom you trust? Trust and financial planning for retirement. *Finance Research Letters, 69*, 106142. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.frl.2024.106142

- What this source is about:
  This article focuses on how trust in financial institutions or advisors shapes retirement planning decisions.
- Why this source fits this citation:
  Your framework has a `willingness` branch built around trust in banks. This source is highly aligned with that idea and is more directly tied to retirement planning than a general trust paper.
- Other viewpoints on the same issue:
  Other literature treats trust as a mediator rather than a primary determinant. Some papers also frame trust as context-dependent and influenced by prior service experience.

### Suggested source 3

Goda, G. S., Levy, M. R., Flaherty Manchester, C., Sojourner, A., Tasoff, J., & Xiao, J. (2023). Are retirement planning tools substitutes or complements to financial capability? *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, 214*, 561-573. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jebo.2023.08.001

- What this source is about:
  This paper examines whether retirement planning tools work as substitutes for financial capability or whether they are more effective when users already have some capability.
- Why this source fits this citation:
  Your sentence includes `ease of use of bank-provided tools` and the opportunity dimension. This paper helps justify the relevance of tools and access conditions in shaping retirement planning behavior.
- Other viewpoints on the same issue:
  Some literature argues digital tools democratize planning and lower barriers. Other studies caution that tools help unevenly and may advantage already capable users.
