One page summary of research proposal

1. Project Title
Exploring the Role of Banks in Improving Financial Retirement Planning through Bank-Led Financial Education Based on the CWO Framework: A Study of Private Sector Employees in Hong Kong

2. Core Question Background
Hong Kong's retirement system for most private sector employees is primarily built around the Mandatory Provident Fund (MPF), which provides only a basic institutional foundation for retirement protection rather than a guaranteed pension outcome (Mandatory Provident Fund Schemes Authority, 2023). By contrast, government servants receive pension protection under the Civil Service Pension Scheme (Civil Service Bureau, n.d.). This difference creates an important context for examining retirement planning among private sector employees, whose long-term financial security depends more heavily on their own planning decisions, savings behaviour, and use of financial services (Chou et al., 2014; Hershey et al., 2010).

As of March 2025, approximately 94% of Hong Kong's workforce was employed in the private sector, while only a small proportion worked in the public sector (Census and Statistics Department, 2025). This makes private sector employees a highly relevant group for research on retirement planning. Existing evidence also suggests that many employees in Hong Kong believe their retirement savings are insufficient, and that financial preparedness remains a concern across income groups (Hwang, 2022). At the same time, banks are important actors in Hong Kong's financial system because they provide MPF-related services, savings products, investment options, and advisory channels that may influence how employees understand and approach retirement planning (Hong Kong Monetary Authority, 2023; Mandatory Provident Fund Schemes Authority, 2023).

Financial education is therefore a potentially important mechanism for improving retirement preparedness. However, the role of banks in delivering retirement-related financial education in Hong Kong has not yet been sufficiently examined, especially in relation to how employees perceive banks, what kind of support they expect, and whether bank-led education can address the barriers they face in retirement planning. This study focuses on private sector employees in Hong Kong and excludes government employees and self-employed individuals because these groups operate under different retirement arrangements and planning conditions.

3. Research Questions
This study aims to examine how bank-led financial education relates to financial retirement planning among private sector employees in Hong Kong and how the CWO framework can be used to structure that analysis. The research questions are:

How do Capability, Willingness, and Opportunity relate to retirement planning among Hong Kong private sector employees?
How do private sector employees perceive the role of banks in financial education for retirement planning, and what kind of role do they expect banks to play?
What financial education practices should banks adopt to better address capability-related, willingness-related, and opportunity-related barriers?

These research questions are explicitly aligned with the CWO framework. The first question examines retirement planning through the three dimensions of capability, willingness, and opportunity. The second question focuses on how employees perceive banks within these dimensions, especially in relation to trust, accessibility, and relevance. The third question uses the findings from the first two questions to identify how banks can design financial education practices that respond to specific CWO-related barriers. This structure ensures that the research questions, data collection, and practical recommendations are conceptually connected.

4. Research Objectives
The study aims to examine how bank-led financial education relates to retirement planning among Hong Kong private sector employees and how the CWO framework can be used to structure the study.

Specifically, the research seeks to:
identify how private sector employees understand retirement planning and bank-led financial education;
examine the main gaps between employees' expectations and the kind of support banks currently provide;
use the CWO framework to organize the survey and interview design;
clarify what role banks are expected to play as retirement educators, information providers, and access points for financial guidance; and
generate practical recommendations for banks on how to design more relevant retirement-related financial education.

Importantly, this project does not claim to test a causal intervention effect. The current design is better suited to examining perceptions, relationships, and design implications than to proving that one intervention directly changes behaviour.

5. Core Concept / Conceptual Framework
The Capability-Willingness-Opportunity (CWO) framework provides the central conceptual lens for this study. It has been used in previous retirement-planning research to explain how behaviour is shaped by a combination of individual resources, motivational factors, and contextual conditions (Blumberg & Pringle, 1982; Hershey et al., 2010; Bandara & Durairatnam, 2025; Han & Sang, 2024). In this study, the framework is used not only as background theory but also as the organizing structure for the whole project.

Capability refers to whether employees have the financial knowledge, practical skills, and confidence needed to understand retirement planning, evaluate options, and make informed decisions. Willingness refers to whether employees are motivated to plan for retirement, trust the information source, and feel ready to take action. Opportunity refers to whether external conditions support or constrain retirement planning, including access to information, time, workplace support, language, and the availability of usable financial channels.

The CWO framework will directly shape the formulation of the research questions, the design of the questionnaire, the development of the interview guide, and the interpretation of the findings. Research Question 1 focuses directly on the three CWO dimensions. Research Question 2 extends the framework by examining whether banks are perceived as credible, accessible, and relevant providers of retirement-related education. Research Question 3 builds on the findings of the first two questions to identify what forms of bank-led financial education are most appropriate for addressing capability-related, willingness-related, and opportunity-related barriers.

This alignment also shapes the research instruments. In the quantitative phase, survey items will be grouped around the three CWO dimensions. Capability-related items will examine retirement knowledge, financial literacy, and self-confidence in making retirement decisions. Willingness-related items will examine motivation to plan, trust in banks, and readiness to act. Opportunity-related items will examine access to information, digital channels, workplace support, and other external conditions that may enable or constrain retirement planning. In the qualitative phase, interview questions will explore how participants interpret these barriers in everyday life and what forms of bank-led support they consider realistic, useful, and trustworthy. In this way, the CWO framework helps structure both the project and the design of the research questions in a coherent and applied manner.

6. Methodology
This study adopts a mixed-methods design combining quantitative and qualitative research among private sector employees in Hong Kong. Quantitative data will identify the overall pattern of relationships among CWO dimensions, retirement planning, and perceptions of bank-led financial education, while qualitative data will help explain why those patterns appear and what they mean in practice (Amabile, 2019).

Quantitative Phase
This study employs a cross-sectional survey design to examine the relationship between CWO dimensions and financial retirement planning behaviours, as well as perceptions of bank-led financial education among private sector employees in Hong Kong. G*Power will be used to estimate the minimum sample size for multiple regression. Using a significance level of alpha = 0.05, a statistical power threshold of 0.80, four predictor variables, and an effect size of f2 = 0.15, the required sample is estimated at approximately 100 respondents (Gefen et al., 2011). Data will be collected through online survey distribution over four to six weeks using community networks, workplace connections, and online channels relevant to the target population.

The questionnaire will be developed through a CWO-based structure. Each section of the survey will correspond to one or more CWO dimensions so that the collected data can be analysed directly in relation to the research questions. The quantitative data will be processed through editing, coding, and classification, followed by descriptive statistical analysis using SPSS or R. Structural relationships may then be examined using PLS-SEM in SmartPLS 4.0.

Qualitative Phase
The second phase uses semi-structured in-depth interviews to explore employees' lived experiences, perceptions of retirement planning, and expectations of banks in more detail. This phase is necessary because retirement planning is shaped not only by measurable variables, but also by trust, habits, experience, and personal context (Amabile, 2019). The interview guide will also follow a CWO-based structure so that the qualitative findings can explain the survey patterns and refine the practical recommendations for banks.

This study uses purposive sampling to select respondents from different industries and job levels (Patton, 1990). Interviews are expected to last 60 to 120 minutes. Regarding sample size, this study draws on the principle of saturation and information power and expects to conduct approximately 10 in-depth interviews (Glaser & Strauss, 1967; Guest et al., 2006; Malterud et al., 2016; Mason, 2010). A small number of focus groups may also be used as a supplementary method to capture group interaction and shared views on retirement-planning barriers and the role of banks (Krueger & Casey, 2009; Morgan, 1996).

Ethical Consideration
The study will avoid collecting personally identifiable information. Participants will be informed that participation is voluntary and that they may withdraw at any time. Because the target population may include speakers of different languages, questionnaire and interview materials may need to be translated carefully to reduce cultural and linguistic bias (Kim & Han, 2004). Data will be stored securely and handled in encrypted form where appropriate.

7. Potential Deliverable
7.1 Academic Thesis: documenting the research findings and implications of the CWO framework for bank-led financial education in Hong Kong before 30 June 2026.
7.2 Policy Toolkit for Banks: a practical package that includes suggestions for financial education design and a simple evaluation framework to assess whether the education is effective.
